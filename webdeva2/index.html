Volleyball

History & Origins of Volleyball

In 1895, William G. Morgan, an instructor at the Young Men's Christian Association (YMCA) Holyoke, Massachusetts, decided blend elements of basketball, baseball, tennis, and handball to create a game. So, he borrowed. From basketball, he took the ball. From tennis the net. From handball, the use of hands and the ability to play off the walls and over hangs. And, from, he took the concept of innings.

Morgan introduced the sport (called mintonette, the original name of volleyball) at the YMCA Physical Director's Conference a year later at Springfield College, Massachusetts. Though it was incomplete with no fixed rules and a format to follow, the sport did enough to win over the delegation and soon became a part of YMCA's wide network throughout the USA with a new name - volley ball (initially it was termed as two words). Volleyball was officially selected to spell as a single word in 1952.

Rules & Violations of Volleyball

    Rules:
  1. Only 6 players on the court at any given time: 3 in the front row and 3 in the back row.
  2. The same player is not able to hit the ball twice in succession (a block is not considered a hit).
  3. A ball hitting the boundary line is considered in.
  4. A ball is out if it hits an antenna, the floor outside of the court (boundary), or the net (and is not able to get over to the other side). The team that has the last contact with the ball loses a point.
  5. It is illegal to catch, hold or throw a ball.
  6. After the serve, front-line players may switch positions at the net.
  7. It is legal to contact the ball with any part of a player's body.

    Violations:
  1. Stepping on or across the service line when serving while contacting the ball.
  2. A back row player attacking a ball inside the front zone (the area inside the 3-metre line) when, at contact, the ball is completely above the net is illegal.
  3. Crossing the court centreline with any part of your body, except for a hand or foot. It is only considered a violation if the entire hand or entire foot crosses the court centreline.
  4. Touching the net with any part of the body while the ball is in play.
  5. Blocking a ball and contacting it if the opponent has not used 3 contacts and has a player making a play for the ball (such as spiking).
